Scope the Problem

Define what your team is actually solving — before you build anything.

Analyzing your scope...
Scope Analysis Live Demo Input
Refined Problem Statement
College students who manage daily prescription medications lack a reliable, low-friction reminder and dispensing system that fits into unpredictable academic schedules — leading to missed doses, inconsistent treatment, and increased health risk.
Key Assumptions to Validate
  • Students are willing to charge or maintain a device consistently
  • The primary barrier is forgetting, not intentional skipping
  • A physical device is preferred over a smartphone-only solution
  • Students have a consistent enough location (dorm room) to place the dispenser
In Scope This Semester
  • Single-compartment prototype for 1–2 medications
  • Time-based alert system (buzzer or LED)
  • Basic logging of dispensed vs. missed
  • User testing with 3–5 student volunteers
Out of Scope
  • Mobile app integration or Bluetooth sync
  • Multi-user or multi-medication management
  • FDA compliance or clinical validation
  • Commercial-grade enclosure or manufacturing
Project Risks
  • Scope creep — the team may be tempted to add app connectivity, which is a separate semester-long project on its own
  • User research gap — assuming you know what students need without actually talking to them first
  • Hardware unreliability late in the semester when prototyping time is limited
3 Questions Your Team Should Discuss This Week
  • Have we talked to at least 5 students who take daily medications? What did we learn that surprised us?
  • What does "working prototype" mean for our project — what must it do to count as a success?
  • Which team member owns the user-testing plan, and when does it need to happen to inform our next design iteration?

Plan the Timeline

Turn a semester into a realistic, milestone-driven schedule your team can actually follow.

Building your milestone plan...
Milestone Plan Live Demo Input
Week-by-Week Milestones
Wks 1–2
Problem discovery — Talk to users, finalize problem statement, submit project proposal
Wks 3–4
Conceptual design — Brainstorm 3+ concepts, sketch rough diagrams, select direction as a team
Wks 5–6
Design specification — Define technical requirements, assign component responsibilities, order parts
Wk 7
Checkpoint ✓
Design Review #1 — Present concept + rationale to instructors
Wks 8–9
First build — Assemble proof-of-concept, expect it to partially fail — that's normal
Wks 10–11
Test + iterate — Run user tests, document failures, refine design
Wk 12
Checkpoint ✓
Design Review #2 — Demonstrate working prototype with evidence of iteration
Wks 13–14
Final polish + report — Document process, finalize demo, write report, present
Bottleneck Warning
Parts ordering and hardware debugging are the most common schedule killers. If parts aren't ordered by Week 5, your build window collapses into two weeks instead of four. Build in a "parts in hand" checkpoint before you finalize your timeline.
Preventing Team Siloing
With 4 members, the risk is that each person owns a component and the team only integrates at the last minute. Assign one "integration owner" who attends every sub-team check-in and is responsible for the full system working together — not just individual parts.

Recover from Failure

A prototype that doesn't work is not a crisis — it's information. Let's figure out what it's telling you.

Diagnosing your failure...
Failure Diagnosis Live Demo Input
Failure Categories Identified
Spec Gap — motor underpowered for load Component selection error — buzzer not validated Missing pre-test — no bench test before assembly User context gap — real environment not simulated
What to Test Next (in order)
  • Measure actual motor torque vs. the load of your full pill container — use the motor datasheet, not assumptions
  • Spec a replacement buzzer at 85–90 dB minimum; test at 3 meters in a room with background noise
  • Rebuild test rig separately before reassembling housing — validate each component in isolation first
How to Talk About This Productively
In your next team meeting, frame this as: "We discovered two testable failure modes — a torque spec mismatch and an output specification gap. Both are fixable. Here's what we know, and here's our plan." Avoid assigning blame to whoever chose the parts. Focus on what the failure revealed about your testing process.
3 Iteration Options
  • Swap the motor — source a higher-torque servo, validate specs first, keep housing design unchanged
  • Redesign the mechanical release — reduce friction in the dispensing mechanism so even a low-torque motor can actuate it
  • Replace buzzer + add visual alert — pair a louder alarm with an LED flash for redundancy across contexts
Do not panic and rebuild everything. You have two specific, diagnosable problems. Fix those. Rebuilding from scratch at this stage almost always makes things worse and wastes the most limited resource you have — time.

Prepare for Review

Walk into your design review with a clear strategy — not just slides.

Building your review strategy...
Review Strategy Live Demo Input
3 Questions Reviewers Are Likely to Ask
  • Q1 "How did you decide on this design direction over alternatives — what did you rule out and why?"
  • Q2 "You mentioned the mechanism jammed — what's your root cause analysis, and how are you fixing it before the final demo?"
  • Q3 "Have you tested this with actual users? What feedback have you received, and how has it changed your design?"
How to Defend Your Choices
For every major decision, be ready to say: "We chose X because it met our constraint of Y, and we considered Z but ruled it out because of [specific reason]." Reviewers are testing whether you made informed, intentional decisions — not whether you chose perfectly. Show your reasoning.
One Weakness to Acknowledge Honestly
Acknowledge the limited user data directly: "We have informal feedback from two users, but our formal testing plan starts next week. We made preliminary design choices based on [rationale] and expect to validate or revise after testing." This is far better than hoping no one notices.
3 Content Priorities for Your Slides or Demo
  • Your process, not just your product — show the iteration: what you tried, what failed, what you changed
  • Your decision criteria — a one-slide matrix or written rationale showing why you made key choices
  • Evidence of forward momentum — your current test plan, next steps, and timeline to a working final demo
Evidence to Bring
Bring your physical prototype — even if imperfect. A real device you can point to (even a broken one) is dramatically more credible than polished slides. If it jams, say: "This is exactly the failure we're diagnosing." Reviewers in engineering know prototypes fail.

Reflection Prompts

Reflection is a skill. These prompts are starting points — the thinking and writing are yours to do.

Generating your reflection prompts...
Reflection Prompts Live Demo Input
Important: These are prompts only. You must write your own reflection in your own words. Submitting AI-generated text as personal reflection is academic dishonesty. Your instructor is evaluating your thinking, not your ability to generate a prompt.
5 Reflection Questions
  • 1.When the motor jammed and the team disagreed about pivoting, what was your instinct — and in hindsight, was it right? What does that tell you about how you make decisions under pressure?
  • 2.As hardware lead, what would you do differently in the first four weeks of the project to prevent the failure that happened in Week 9?
  • 3.The team chose to stay with the mechanical mechanism rather than pivot. How did you personally contribute to or resist that decision, and what were the consequences?
  • 4.What did you assume about your users that turned out to be wrong — and how did you find out?
  • 5.If you had to describe your team's design process to a new student starting this course, what would you tell them to do differently?
3 Sentence Starters
The moment I realized our process needed to change was when...
As hardware lead, the decision I most struggled with was... because...
The failure in Week 9 changed how I think about [testing / teamwork / design] because...
